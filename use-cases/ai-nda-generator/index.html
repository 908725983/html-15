The Problem

What Is an AI NDA Generator?

Traditional NDA drafting is a bottleneck hiding in plain sight. The average in-house legal team spends 20 hours per week drafting, customizing, and reviewing non-disclosure agreements — the most common contract type in business. Each NDA takes 45-90 minutes: find the right NDA template, customize party names and scope, check that governing law is correct, align non-compete and non-solicit clauses with the jurisdiction, add standard carve-outs, cross-reference definitions, format, and send. For a legal team handling 15-20 NDAs per week, that is nearly half of a full-time attorney's capacity — spent on documents that are 90% boilerplate. An AI NDA generator changes this by automating the initial drafting process while keeping attorneys in control of every final agreement.

Template libraries (Practical Law, LexisNexis, Westlaw) and DIY legal platforms (LegalZoom, Rocket Lawyer) promised to speed this up, but they only provide static NDA templates. You still manually customize every clause, verify jurisdiction alignment, and check for internal consistency. A New York NDA template used for a California deal creates unenforceable non-compete provisions. A mutual NDA template does not properly convert to a unilateral NDA. A clause added in Section 3 conflicts with a definition in Section 1. These errors are caught — or worse, missed — during manual review. Industry surveys indicate that a significant share of in-house counsel cite contract drafting and review as their top productivity drain, and many express interest in AI-assisted drafting for jurisdiction-appropriate, routine agreements. Contract drafting automation and legal document automation tools — specifically an AI NDA generator — close this gap by generating complete, jurisdiction-specific NDA drafts from your templates and clause library in seconds rather than hours, for attorney review and finalization.

What is the difference between a mutual NDA and a unilateral NDA?
A mutual NDA (also called a bilateral NDA) protects the confidential information of both parties — each party's obligations, carve-outs, and liability provisions are independently defined. Both parties agree to protect each other's confidential information under the same agreement. A unilateral NDA (also called a one-way NDA) protects only one party's confidential information — the receiving party agrees to protect the disclosing party's information, but not vice versa. Unilateral NDAs are common when only one party is sharing sensitive information, such as a company sharing proprietary data with a consultant or contractor. COCO's AI NDA generator handles both types and can auto-convert between them — generating a one-way NDA from a mutual NDA template with the proper structural changes to obligation clauses, carve-outs, and liability provisions.
What is a one-way NDA?
A one-way NDA (also called a unilateral NDA) is a non-disclosure agreement where only one party — the disclosing party — shares confidential information, and the other party — the receiving party — agrees to protect it. This is different from a mutual NDA where both parties exchange and protect confidential information. One-way NDAs are commonly used when a company shares proprietary information with a consultant, contractor, or potential service provider where only one direction of information flow is expected. COCO's AI NDA generator handles one-way NDAs automatically — generating the correct clause architecture with obligations, carve-outs, and liability provisions structured for unilateral protection rather than mutual exchange.
How long does an NDA last?
The typical NDA term — the period during which confidential information can be shared under the agreement — ranges from 1 to 5 years, with 2-3 years being the most common for commercial NDAs. The confidentiality obligations themselves often survive beyond the term: many NDAs specify that the duty to protect confidential information continues for a defined period after the agreement ends (commonly 2-5 years), and trade secrets are typically protected indefinitely. COCO's AI NDA generator applies industry-standard term conventions by default but customizes the duration to your specific deal requirements and your organization's preferred positions in your legal playbook.
Do NDAs expire?
Yes, most NDAs have an expiration. The term of the NDA — the period during which confidential information can be shared — typically expires after 1-5 years. However, the confidentiality obligations often survive expiration: the receiving party must continue to protect confidential information disclosed during the active term for an additional period (commonly 2-5 years post-expiration). Trade secrets are generally protected indefinitely, even after the NDA expires. COCO's AI NDA generator automatically includes appropriate survival clauses for confidentiality obligations and trade secret protection, customized to the governing law and your legal playbook preferences.
Do non-disclosure agreements need to be notarized?
No, non-disclosure agreements generally do not need to be notarized to be legally enforceable. NDAs are governed by contract law, and a valid contract typically requires only offer, acceptance, consideration, and mutual assent — not notarization. Electronic signatures are widely accepted for NDAs under the ESIGN Act (US) and eIDAS (EU). However, in certain jurisdictions or for specific types of agreements (such as those involving real property or certain international transactions), notarization may be recommended or required. COCO's AI NDA generator produces standard commercial NDAs designed for electronic execution. For specific jurisdictional requirements or unusual deal structures, consult your legal counsel.
